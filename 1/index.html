1

Why we are having this conversation

The situation

Evergreen Golf Partners — a PE-adjacent operator founded in 2023 that acquired Blackthorn (Dec 2024), Governors Towne (Jan 2025), and Somerby (Feb 2026) — is in discussions to acquire Reunion.

Current ownership has opened the door for a member-led acquisition at an offer competitive with Evergreen's. Planning assumption: the club and associated extra land trade for roughly $10 million, a figure consistent with a money-losing asset (~$1M/yr cash burn) valued closer to hard-asset + turnaround value than EBITDA multiple.

Valuation bracket from our prior analysis: floor $8M · likely $10–13M · stretch $14–16M+ if land carries real standalone value.

What Evergreen is likely to do

Their public playbook is consistent: professionalize service and systems, standardize the back office (Clubspot, centralized reporting), activate non-golf amenities, push membership growth and event revenue, and — while they don't say it out loud — methodical dues and assessment increases over 3–5 years to support capex.

None of that is inherently bad. The question is whether members want those decisions made for them by an out-of-state operator optimizing a portfolio, or by them through a local ownership vehicle.

Somerby members ratified at 96% Pro: capital & expertise Con: loss of local control

Our alternative, in one sentence

Recapitalize Reunion as a member-owned club using a tiered equity-and-non-equity membership structure that raises the bulk of the purchase price from members who want ownership, fills the gap with conservative debt, and leaves day-to-day use rights for current and future members largely unchanged.

2

Design principles

Everything in this document flows from six principles. They exist to get us through a 60% member vote and survive the first three years.

Principle 1

Attractive to whales without being oligarchic

Patron-tier backers get prestige, amenities, and refund priority — not weighted votes. One equity membership, one vote. Prevents a political backlash that kills the deal.

Principle 2

No forced check from current members

Existing members are automatically grandfathered into a comparable non-equity class at no additional cost. Equity is an option, never a requirement. This is the single most important design choice for the vote.

Principle 3

Dues discipline

Dues schedule for non-equity members is capped for 24–36 months post-close. Equity holders absorb the turnaround risk — that's what equity is.

Principle 4

Scarcity-backed equity value

Hard caps on total equity memberships. Once sold out, new members enter as non-equity until an equity seat is reissued. Protects the long-term value of what founders are buying.

Principle 5

Club reissuance, not open-market resale

Departing equity members return their membership to the club, which resells it and pays out under a defined formula. Avoids a gray market and keeps admissions under member control.

Principle 6

Transparent economics

Every member gets the same pro forma, same cap table, same debt stack, same turnaround plan. If we can't explain it cleanly on a single page, it won't pass.

Governance & voting

Toggle between governance models to see the trade-offs. Our recommendation is the middle option — one equity member, one vote, with reserved board seats for the patron tier.

Recommended

One equity membership, one vote

All equity members — Patron, Founder, Charter, Legacy — vote equally. The Patron tier gets amenity privileges, founder recognition, and refund priority, not extra votes.

Reserved board seats ensure larger contributors have a voice: e.g. 1 Patron seat, 2 Founder seats, 2 Charter, 2 at-large.

What equity members vote on

  • • Election of directors (annual)
  • • Amendment of bylaws & articles
  • • Sale of substantially all club assets (supermajority)
  • • Merger / conversion / dissolution (supermajority)
  • • Capital assessments over a threshold ($2,500/member/yr)
  • • Debt issuance over a threshold ($1M)

Pros

Democratic — passes 60% more easily

Aligned with IRS Rev. Rul. precedent for member-owned clubs

Avoids "controlled oligarchy" perception

Cons

Less appealing to the $250k check writer who wants control

Factions can form along tier lines anyway

Not recommended

Votes weighted by capital contribution

Each dollar of equity contribution = one vote (e.g. $250k Patron = 10x the vote of a $25k Legacy).

Pros

Very attractive to large backers

Accelerates fundraising

Cons

Triggers securities-law scrutiny — this starts to look like an investment, not a club

Politically toxic at the 60% member vote

Most clubs abandon this within 5 years under member pressure

Tax treatment of member-owned exempt clubs gets complicated

Workable but complex

Dual-class: Founder Control Class vs. Regular Equity

Patron + Founder equity forms a "Class A" that controls initial board composition for a set period (e.g. 5 years), after which governance converts to one-member-one-vote across all equity.

Pros

Gives whales what they want (control through turnaround)

Sunset clause makes it politically sellable

Ensures experienced operators steer the rebuild

Cons

Harder to pass 60% — members don't like founder control, even with sunset

Risk of founders extending sunset via bylaw amendment

Requires very careful drafting

Refund & reissuance mechanics

Probably the most operationally consequential design decision. If we overpromise refunds, we crush future cash flow. If we underpromise, fewer people write the founder check.

Model How it works Pros Cons Verdict
Fully refundable on reissuance Member resigns → club waits for new buyer → 100% paid back to departing member Most attractive to initial check-writers Kills cash flow when membership cycles; clubs often have to defer refunds 3–7 years or permanently Avoid
Partial refundable on reissuance (recommended) 80% (Patron / Founder) → 70% (Charter) → 50–60% (Legacy) of then-current tier price Balance between attractiveness & cash preservation Requires clean accounting for "then-current" price; needs waitlist admin Use this
Non-refundable equity Initiation is a one-way contribution. You have rights while active; none upon resignation. Cleanest cash flow; simpler accounting; no waitlist drama Hard to sell at $100k+. Members feel like they bought something they can't sell. Only for Legacy/Junior tier
Club sells 2-for-1 before reissuance Until debt is retired, for every 1 resigned membership reissued to refund a departing member, club sells 1 additional new membership for club account Lets club recapitalize over time while honoring refund obligations Only works if there's demand for new memberships Layer on top of #2

Recommended refund stack

  • ▸ Partial refund on reissuance: Patron/Founder 80%, Charter 70%, Legacy 50%
  • ▸ "Then-current" tier price — no speculative upside for departing members
  • ▸ 2-for-1 reissuance rule: club sells one new seat for club account for every resigned seat reissued, until bank debt is fully retired
  • ▸ Maximum payout cadence: club pays refund within 12 months of reissuance, not upon resignation
  • ▸ Hard cap on simultaneous refunds: no more than 10% of equity membership cycles out per year
8

Winning the 60% vote

The deal doesn't pass because the structure is elegant. It passes because enough members personally feel the downside of Evergreen and the upside of ownership, and because nobody thinks they're being forced into something.

Three questions every member will ask

  1. 1. "Do I have to pay anything?"
    Answer: No. Your existing membership is automatically grandfathered into a comparable non-equity class. Equity is an option for those who want ownership and voting rights.
  2. 2. "Will my dues go up?"
    Answer: Dues for non-equity members are capped for 24–36 months. After that, increases require a board vote with member notice — same governance protection that exists today.
  3. 3. "What happens if this doesn't work?"
    Answer: Worst case, the club is sold to another operator. In that case, equity holders absorb losses first, not non-equity members. That's what equity means.

Campaign architecture

  1. Phase 1 — Steering committee forms (month 0). 10–15 members including the deal organizers, 2–3 influential tenured members, a CPA, and ideally a current/former board member.
  2. Phase 2 — Counsel retained. Club law + tax + real estate. Non-negotiable.
  3. Phase 3 — Non-binding LOI with seller. Locks in exclusivity while structure is finalized.
  4. Phase 4 — Anchor capital secured (month 2–4). Patron and Founder tier commitments papered before going wider. Typically 50–70% of target equity needs to be soft-circled here.
  5. Phase 5 — Town halls (month 4–5). Three sessions — weekday morning (retirees), weekday evening (working families), weekend (corporate). Somerby got to 96% by running small-group town halls, not one big town hall.
  6. Phase 6 — Vote (month 5–6). 30 days' notice. Proxy voting supported.
  7. Phase 7 — Close & transition (month 7–9). Close the deal. First 90 days post-close is critical: no surprise changes to anything member-facing.

Honest risks

What could kill this. We owe the steering committee and the member base a clear-eyed view.

Insufficient anchor capital

If fewer than 5–10 families write $100k+ checks, the whole capital stack falls apart and we end up with more debt than the club can service against $1M of ongoing losses. Mitigation: confirm soft commitments covering 50–70% of equity target before signing LOI.

Evergreen outbids us meaningfully

If Evergreen goes to $13–15M, member-financed deal economics get very hard. Mitigation: lock in a right of first refusal or matching right with ownership before they sign with Evergreen. Also accept that we may not be the highest bidder; current ownership may accept a lower member bid if the relationship is good and the transition is cleaner.

Turnaround takes longer than $2M working capital covers

$1M/year burn × 2 years = $2M. If operations don't improve in 24 months, we're back at the members for a capital call. Mitigation: hire experienced GM before close (not after), build conservative 36-month operating plan with explicit triggers for corrective action, build capex reserve separately so it isn't eaten by operating losses.

Securities law exposure

Selling "equity memberships" can, depending on structure, look like issuing securities — especially if we promise anything approximating an investment return. Mitigation: club counsel + securities counsel review structure; we lean into "use rights + governance rights" language and avoid any language suggesting investment return. Traditional member-owned club structures have well-understood safe harbors.

Vote fails between 40–59% YES

The worst case: we campaign, alienate seller, and don't get 60%. Mitigation: internal whip count before calling the vote. If we can't count 60% of soft-yeses plus 10% margin 30 days out, we pull the vote, re-structure, or withdraw.

Tax / nonprofit exempt status complications

Reunion's current tax status matters a lot. A 501(c)(7) exempt social club has strict rules on non-member revenue, and converting entity structure can disrupt exempt status. Mitigation: tax counsel from day one. Don't commit to structure until tax treatment is confirmed.

Sports membership voice not captured

This deck focuses on the 544 golf primaries because that's the data we have. Sports-only members are estimated at 50–100+ based on the user's recollection and also vote. Under-engaging them risks losing their block. Mitigation: get the sports roster early, segment them, include a Sports/Social equity conversion path.

Post-close governance drift

Member-owned clubs often start great, then drift into board factionalism, slow decisions, and deferred capex because committees can't agree on assessments. Mitigation: clear bylaws on dues escalation authority (board-set within defined bands, member-vote above), well-defined CEO/GM authority, term limits on directors, no more than 9 board seats.
