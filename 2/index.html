Why we are having this conversation

The situation

On April 15, 2026, RG&CC ownership announced Evergreen Partners as the chosen new owner and — as required by the Membership Plan — triggered the members' one-time opportunity to organize and negotiate a purchase. We have 45 days from notice (deadline May 30) to execute a purchase agreement, and 90 days total (July 14) to close.

Planning range: $10M – $25M, with $20 million as the default expectation. The lower end reflects pure money-losing-club valuation on hard-asset and turnaround value. The $20M+ end reflects the trophy quality of the clubhouse, the strategic value of the club to the surrounding community, the extra land in the deal, and a competitive bid against Evergreen.

Working assumption for v0.2: senior debt and seller paper are tentatively secured pending PA. The equity raise from members and the precise purchase price are the two gating variables — the calculator below shows how they trade off.

What Evergreen is likely to do

Their public playbook is consistent: professionalize service and systems, standardize the back office (Clubspot, centralized reporting), activate non-golf amenities, push membership growth and event revenue, and — while they don't say it out loud — methodical dues and assessment increases over 3–5 years to support capex.

None of that is inherently bad. The question is whether members want those decisions made for them by an out-of-state operator optimizing a portfolio, or by them through a local ownership vehicle.

Somerby members ratified at 96% Pro: capital & expertise Con: loss of local control

Our alternative, in one sentence

Recapitalize Reunion as a member-owned club using a tiered equity-and-non-equity membership structure that raises the bulk of the purchase price from members who want ownership, fills the gap with conservative debt, and leaves day-to-day use rights for current and future members largely unchanged.

Design principles

Everything in this document flows from six principles. They exist to get us through a 60% member vote and survive the first three years.

Principle 1

Attractive to whales without being oligarchic

Patron-tier backers get prestige, amenities, and refund priority — not weighted votes. One equity membership, one vote. Prevents a political backlash that kills the deal.

Principle 2

No forced check from current members

Existing members are automatically grandfathered into a non-equity class at no additional cost. Existing members can ALSO buy in to equity at the entry-level $10k tier any time over the next 10 years (financing available). New members joining the club after close MUST buy equity. This is the single most important design choice for the vote.

Principle 3

Dues discipline

Dues schedule for non-equity members is capped for 24–36 months post-close. Equity holders absorb the turnaround risk — that's what equity is.

Principle 4

Scarcity-backed equity value

Hard caps on total equity memberships. Once sold out, new members enter as non-equity until an equity seat is reissued. Protects the long-term value of what founders are buying.

Principle 5

Club reissuance, not open-market resale

Departing equity members return their membership to the club, which resells it and pays out under a defined formula. Avoids a gray market and keeps admissions under member control.

Principle 6

Transparent economics

Every member gets the same pro forma, same cap table, same debt stack, same turnaround plan. If we can't explain it cleanly on a single page, it won't pass.

Governance & voting

The recommended model is one equity membership, one vote — but only $50k+ tiers carry board eligibility. The board proposes; members ratify. Proxy voting is supported. Toggle between models to see the trade-offs.

Recommended

One equity membership, one vote

All equity members vote equally — Patron, Founder, Charter, Legacy, and Junior. Patron and Founder get amenity privileges, founder recognition, and refund priority, not extra votes.

Board eligibility is restricted to $50k+ tiers (Charter, Founder, Patron). Legacy ($25k) and Junior ($12k) members vote on everything but cannot serve on the board. This keeps board service connected to meaningful financial commitment without creating weighted votes.

Reserved seat structure: 1 Patron, 2 Founder, 3 Charter, 1 at-large (any equity tier). Total 7 directors.

Board vs. member-vote authority

Best-practice split: the board proposes and operates; members ratify the consequential things. The board cannot bind the membership unilaterally on any of the items at right.

Board decides directly (no member vote)

  • • Day-to-day operations, hiring, vendors
  • • Annual budget (within bands set by membership)
  • • Routine capex up to $250k
  • • Member admissions and discipline
  • • GM compensation

Members ratify (one equity = one vote)

  • • Election of directors (annual)
  • • Amendment of bylaws or articles
  • • Sale of substantially all club assets (supermajority)
  • • Merger, conversion, dissolution (supermajority)
  • • Capital assessments over $2,500/member/yr
  • • New debt issuance over $1M
  • • Annual dues schedule outside the pre-agreed escalator band
New rule

Proxy voting

Any equity member may designate another equity member as their proxy in writing for any vote. Proxies are single-vote (no proxy can carry more than 5 other members' votes, to prevent vote-pooling).

  • • Written, signed (electronic OK), member-name specific
  • • Valid for the named vote only — no standing proxies
  • • Filed with the club secretary 24 hours before vote
  • • Revocable any time before vote is called

Practical effect: snowbirds, traveling executives, and members who can't attend in person still count toward the 60% threshold. This materially helps quorum.

New rule

24-month new-member voting waiting period

New members joining the club after close — i.e. members who buy in as $12k Junior Equity or $25k Legacy Equity post-acquisition — have full club use rights from day one but cannot vote on member-vote matters until their 24-month anniversary of membership.

  • • Protects the existing equity base from vote dilution by short-tenure new members
  • • Preserves long-term incentive alignment — voters have skin in the game over time
  • • Existing members at close are NOT subject to the waiting period; they vote immediately
  • • Members upgrading tiers don't reset their clock

Common in member-owned clubs to prevent rapid-onboarding factions from changing direction. 24 months is the typical balance point — long enough to discourage gaming, short enough to feel fair.

Not recommended

Votes weighted by capital contribution

Each dollar of equity contribution = one vote (e.g. $250k Patron = 10x the vote of a $25k Legacy).

Pros

Very attractive to large backers

Accelerates fundraising

Cons

Triggers securities-law scrutiny — this starts to look like an investment, not a club

Politically toxic at the 60% member vote

Most clubs abandon this within 5 years under member pressure

Tax treatment of member-owned exempt clubs gets complicated

Workable but complex

Dual-class: Founder Control Class vs. Regular Equity

Patron + Founder equity forms a "Class A" that controls initial board composition for a set period (e.g. 5 years), after which governance converts to one-member-one-vote across all equity.

Pros

Gives whales what they want (control through turnaround)

Sunset clause makes it politically sellable

Ensures experienced operators steer the rebuild

Cons

Harder to pass 60% — members don't like founder control, even with sunset

Risk of founders extending sunset via bylaw amendment

Requires very careful drafting

Refund & reissuance mechanics

Probably the most operationally consequential design decision. If we overpromise refunds, we crush future cash flow. If we underpromise, fewer people write the founder check.

Tier Price Vote? Board eligible? Refund on reissuance
Patron Founder $250,000 ✓ Yes (1 vote) ✓ Yes 80% of then-current price · top priority
Founder Equity $100,000 ✓ Yes (1 vote) ✓ Yes 80% of then-current price · second priority
Charter Equity $50,000 ✓ Yes (1 vote) ✓ Yes 70% of then-current price · standard queue
Legacy Equity $25,000 ✓ Yes (1 vote) ✕ No 50% of then-current price · lowest tier with refund
Junior Equity New $12,000 ✓ Yes (1 vote) ✕ No ✕ Non-refundable

Refund stack — at a glance

  • ▸ $250k Patron / $100k Founder: 80% on reissuance
  • ▸ $50k Charter: 70% on reissuance
  • ▸ $25k Legacy: 50% on reissuance — lowest refund tier
  • ▸ $12k Junior: non-refundable
  • ▸ Refund based on then-current tier price (no speculative upside)
  • ▸ Refund paid within 12 months of reissuance, not upon resignation
  • ▸ Hard cap: ≤10% of equity memberships cycle out per year

Path-upward rule

Any equity member can move up tiers when slots are available. Move up by paying the difference between current tier and target tier price. Tier upgrades:

  • • Inherit refund priority of the new (higher) tier
  • • Begin earning board eligibility immediately on reaching $50k+
  • • Do NOT reset the 24-month voting waiting period (it doesn't apply to upgrades — only to brand-new joins)
  • • Subject to current cap availability

Junior Equity → Legacy → Charter → Founder → Patron. Most members will start where they can afford and grow into higher tiers.

Reissuance mechanics — recommended

  • ▸ Members resign → club places membership in queue → reissues to next eligible buyer → pays refund within 12 months of reissuance
  • ▸ 2-for-1 reissuance rule: until bank debt is fully retired, for every 1 resigned membership reissued, the club may sell 1 additional new membership for club account. This funds debt paydown without raising assessments.
  • ▸ Junior tier ($12k) does NOT enter the queue — those seats are simply absorbed by the club and reissued to new joiners.
  • ▸ All resignations require 90-day written notice. Refund accrual begins on the reissuance date, not the resignation date.
8

Winning the 60% vote

The deal doesn't pass because the structure is elegant. It passes because enough members personally feel the downside of Evergreen and the upside of ownership, and because nobody thinks they're being forced into something.

Three questions every member will ask

  1. 1. "Do I have to pay anything?"
    Answer: No. Your existing membership is automatically grandfathered into a non-equity class at no cost. Equity is an option — you can also buy in to the entry-level $10k tier any time over the next 10 years, with financing available.
  2. 2. "Can I move up if I start small?"
    Answer: Yes. Every tier has a path upward. Buy in at $12k or $25k, then upgrade to $50k Charter, $100k Founder, or $250k Patron when slots are available — you simply pay the difference between tiers. Board eligibility unlocks at $50k.
  3. 3. "What happens if this doesn't work?"
    Answer: Worst case, the club is sold to another operator. In that case, equity holders absorb losses first, not non-equity members. That's what equity means — and it's why grandfathered current members are protected.

Honest risks

What could kill this. We owe the steering committee and the member base a clear-eyed view.

Insufficient anchor capital

If fewer than 5–10 families write $100k+ checks, the whole capital stack falls apart and we end up with more debt than the club can service against $1M of ongoing losses. Mitigation: confirm soft commitments covering 50–70% of equity target before signing LOI.

Evergreen outbids us meaningfully

If Evergreen goes to $13–15M, member-financed deal economics get very hard. Mitigation: lock in a right of first refusal or matching right with ownership before they sign with Evergreen. Also accept that we may not be the highest bidder; current ownership may accept a lower member bid if the relationship is good and the transition is cleaner.

Turnaround takes longer than $2M working capital covers

$1M/year burn × 2 years = $2M. If operations don't improve in 24 months, we're back at the members for a capital call. Mitigation: hire experienced GM before close (not after), build conservative 36-month operating plan with explicit triggers for corrective action, build capex reserve separately so it isn't eaten by operating losses.

Securities law exposure

Selling "equity memberships" can, depending on structure, look like issuing securities — especially if we promise anything approximating an investment return. Mitigation: club counsel + securities counsel review structure; we lean into "use rights + governance rights" language and avoid any language suggesting investment return. Traditional member-owned club structures have well-understood safe harbors.

Vote fails between 40–59% YES

The worst case: we campaign, alienate seller, and don't get 60%. Mitigation: internal whip count before calling the vote. If we can't count 60% of soft-yeses plus 10% margin 30 days out, we pull the vote, re-structure, or withdraw.

Tax / nonprofit exempt status complications

Reunion's current tax status matters a lot. A 501(c)(7) exempt social club has strict rules on non-member revenue, and converting entity structure can disrupt exempt status. Mitigation: tax counsel from day one. Don't commit to structure until tax treatment is confirmed.

Sports membership voice not captured

This deck focuses on the 544 golf primaries because that's the data we have. Sports-only members are estimated at 50–100+ based on the user's recollection and also vote. Under-engaging them risks losing their block. Mitigation: get the sports roster early, segment them, include a Sports/Social equity conversion path.

Post-close governance drift

Member-owned clubs often start great, then drift into board factionalism, slow decisions, and deferred capex because committees can't agree on assessments. Mitigation: clear bylaws on dues escalation authority (board-set within defined bands, member-vote above), well-defined CEO/GM authority, term limits on directors, no more than 9 board seats.
